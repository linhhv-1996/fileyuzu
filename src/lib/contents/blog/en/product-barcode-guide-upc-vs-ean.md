## What Is a Retail Product Barcode?

Commercial product barcodes operate within a global tracking infrastructure managed by a non-profit international organization called **GS1**. These barcodes do not explicitly store data like retail prices, item descriptions, or manufacturing dates. Instead, they encode a single, unique global identification number string known as a **GTIN (Global Trade Item Number)**.

When a store clerk scans your item at a checkout counter, the point-of-sale (POS) terminal reads that GTIN sequence, references it against the store's internal database, and matches it to your specific product listing and price entry. Without an official, verified GTIN barcode, online platforms like Amazon and retail giants like Walmart will reject your inventory automatically.

## UPC-A vs. EAN-13: Mapping the Global Standards

The global retail supply chain relies on two primary barcode symbology frameworks: **UPC-A** and **EAN-13**.


```
UPC-A:  [12 Numerical Digits] -> Dominant in North America (US and Canada)
EAN-13: [13 Numerical Digits] -> Standard Across Europe, Asia, and Globally
```

### UPC-A (Universal Product Code)
UPC-A is a **12-digit** barcode system. Developed in the United States in the 1970s, it remains the standard retail format across North America. If you are manufacturing goods intended to be distributed exclusively inside the US or Canada, UPC-A is your native retail framework.

### EAN-13 (European Article Number)
EAN-13 is a **13-digit** barcode system. It was engineered as a global expansion of the original UPC standard to accommodate specific geographic country prefixes. EAN-13 is the default retail format across Europe, Asia, Latin America, Australia, and Africa.

## Global Logistics Expansion: Demystifying the JAN Code

If your e-commerce brand or manufacturing operation scales into the Japanese market, you will encounter the term **JAN Code (Japanese Article Number)**.

New sellers often make the mistake of assuming a JAN code requires building an entirely separate barcoding infrastructure. In reality, a JAN code is simply an **EAN-13 barcode** that uses Japan's designated GS1 country prefixes.

Every official JAN sequence begins with the prefix digits **45** or **49**. If you are an American company exporting goods to Tokyo or Osaka, you do not need to overhaul your labeling architecture. ## The Correct Way to Acquire Official Product Barcodes

To ensure your inventory codes are unique and legally tied to your brand registry, you must follow the official procurement process:

1. **Apply with GS1:** Navigate to the official GS1 portal for your home country (such as GS1 US) and submit a corporate membership application. Upon approval, you will receive an exclusive GS1 Company Prefix.
2. **Assign Unique GTIN Sequences:** Use your company prefix to allocate distinct item numbers for your products. Keep in mind that every single variation requires its own unique number string—a red t-shirt and a blue t-shirt need completely separate GTIN numbers.

## FAQ

### Can I use a 12-digit UPC barcode to sell items outside of North America?
Yes. Virtually all modern point-of-sale scanners across Europe and Asia are cross-compatible and can read both 12-digit UPC configurations and 13-digit EAN layouts. However, if your target market is international, establishing EAN-13 from the start is highly recommended.

### What is the difference between a GTIN string and a barcode image?
The GTIN is the actual digital number string identifying your asset within global databases. The barcode image is simply the machine-readable graphic layout (the vertical black lines and white spaces) that allows scanners to instantly read that underlying GTIN sequence.

### Is a Japanese JAN code structurally different from an EAN barcode?
No. A JAN code is structurally identical to a standard EAN-13 barcode. The only difference is that a JAN code always begins with the numerical prefixes 45 or 49, indicating that the item is registered within the Japanese retail system.

### Can I buy cheap product barcodes from third-party reseller sites?
While reseller websites sell cheap unverified barcodes, using them is risky. Retail networks like Amazon now check barcode databases directly against official GS1 records. If the company prefix listed on your barcode doesn't match your brand name, your listings can be permanently removed.

### Which image format should I use to export my retail product barcodes?
You should export your assets as **SVG** files if you are sending them to a professional graphic designer to integrate into custom product packaging boxes or labels. Use high-resolution **PNG** files if you are printing basic stickers or internal logistics sheets.
